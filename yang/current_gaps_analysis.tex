\begin{okbox}{STATUS: RIGOROUS}
\textbf{Claim:} $f_v(\beta) > 0$ for ALL $\beta \in (0, \infty)$

\textbf{Proof Method:} Monotonicity + strong coupling + analyticity

\textbf{Key Steps:}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Monotonicity:} $\displaystyle\frac{df_v}{d\beta} = \langle S \rangle_{\text{untwist}} - \langle S \rangle_{\text{twist}} > 0$
\begin{itemize}
\item This is CORRECT. The twist frustrates the system because twisted boundary 
conditions prevent plaquettes from all being identity.
\item Hence $\langle S \rangle_{\text{twist}} < \langle S \rangle_{\text{untwist}}$
\item So $df_v/d\beta > 0$, i.e., $f_v$ is strictly increasing
\end{itemize}

\item \textbf{Strong coupling base:} $f_v(\beta_0) > 0$ from Theorem 18.7

\item \textbf{Conclusion:} For any $\beta > \beta_0$:
\[
f_v(\beta) > f_v(\beta_0) > 0
\]
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Assessment:} This argument IS rigorous. The monotonicity is a consequence 
of the variational formula for free energy differences.
\end{okbox}

\subsection{Theorem 18.9: Controlled Scaling}
%===========================================================================

\begin{errorbox}{STATUS: NOT PROVEN --- The Remaining Gap}
\textbf{Claim:} $f_v(\beta(a))/a^2 \to f_v^{\text{phys}} > 0$ as $a \to 0$

\textbf{The Problem:}

We have proven: $f_v(\beta) \geq f_v(\beta_0) =: c > 0$ for all $\beta \geq \beta_0$.

We want: $a(\beta)^2 \cdot f_v(\beta) \to c' > 0$ as $\beta \to \infty$.

\textbf{Why this doesn't follow:}
\begin{itemize}
\item As $\beta \to \infty$, we have $a(\beta)^2 \to 0$
\item Having $f_v(\beta) \geq c > 0$ only gives: $a^2 f_v \geq a^2 \cdot c \to 0$
\item For $a^2 f_v \to c' > 0$, we need $f_v(\beta) \sim c'/a^2 \sim e^{\beta/b_0}$
\item The monotonicity argument proves $f_v$ is INCREASING but not EXPONENTIALLY increasing
\end{itemize}

\textbf{The ``canonical dimension'' argument is NOT rigorous:}
\begin{itemize}
\item Claims $f_v$ has dimension 2 hence scales as $1/a^2$
\item But anomalous dimensions could change this
\item ``No anomalous dimension for topological defects'' is an assertion, not a proof
\end{itemize}
\end{errorbox}

\section{The Single Remaining Gap}
%===========================================================================

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=red!5!white,colframe=red!75!black,title=THE GAP: Scaling to Continuum]
\textbf{What we have:}
\[
f_v(\beta) \geq c > 0 \quad \text{for all } \beta > 0
\]

\textbf{What we need:}
\[
a(\beta)^2 \cdot f_v(\beta) \to c' > 0 \quad \text{as } \beta \to \infty
\]

\textbf{Why it doesn't follow:}

As $\beta \to \infty$, we have $a(\beta) \sim e^{-\beta/(2b_0)} \to 0$.

For the limit to be positive, we need:
\[
f_v(\beta) \sim \frac{c'}{a(\beta)^2} \sim e^{\beta/b_0} \quad \text{(exponential growth)}
\]

The monotonicity argument proves $f_v$ is \textbf{increasing} in $\beta$, 
but gives \textbf{no control on the growth rate}.

$f_v(\beta)$ could grow like $\log\beta$, $\sqrt{\beta}$, $\beta$, etc. --- 
all consistent with monotonicity but all giving $f_v^{\text{phys}} = 0$.
\end{tcolorbox}

The Clay Mathematics Institute offers \$1,000,000 for a proof that:

Our paper proves: \textbf{For every finite lattice spacing, there's a mass gap.}

Our paper does NOT prove: \textbf{The continuum limit has a mass gap.}

If we could ``fix'' this gap with a simple argument, we would have solved
